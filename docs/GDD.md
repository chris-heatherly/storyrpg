## 1) Vision and Core Concept

### What Is StoryRPG?

StoryRPG is an interactive fiction application that combines the emotional storytelling of a visual novel with the systemic depth of a tabletop role-playing game. What makes it unique is that its stories are generated by artificial intelligence, using a sophisticated multi-agent pipeline that produces coherent, emotionally resonant narrative content.

The app presents stories as full-screen, image-backed reading experiences where the player makes meaningful choices that shape who their character becomes. Think of it as a "choose your own adventure" book that has been elevated with AI-generated prose, illustrations, and optional voice narration.

### The Central Design Question

**How do we make AI-generated narrative feel authored, intentional, and emotionally coherent instead of random?**

This question drives every design decision. AI-generated text is prone to drift, inconsistency, and emotional flatness. StoryRPG addresses this through:

- **Structured generation:** Stories are not generated as freeform text. Instead, specialized AI agents build world bibles, character profiles, story outlines, scene prose, and choices in a carefully sequenced pipeline with validation at each step.
- **Fiction-first presentation:** The player never sees numbers, dice rolls, or stat sheets. All game mechanics are expressed through narrative prose and character behavior.
- **Identity through pressure:** Choices are designed to reveal who the player's character is becoming, not to optimize a build or solve a puzzle.
- **Bounded creativity:** The AI operates within guardrails (structure templates, word count limits, consequence budgets, quality validators) that prevent the common failure modes of generative content.

### What StoryRPG Is Not

- It is not an open-world sandbox with infinite freeform text generation.
- It is not a chatbot where you type actions and the AI responds.
- It is not a game with visible RPG stats, experience points, or level-up screens.
- It is not a multiplayer or online game (it is currently a local-first, single-player application).

## 2) Design Philosophy: Fiction-First

### What Fiction-First Means

"Fiction-first" is the foundational design principle. It means that the player's experience is expressed entirely through the story world. Behind the scenes, the game tracks numbers, probabilities, relationship scores, and identity vectors, but the player never directly sees any of these.

**Examples of fiction-first intent:**

| Behind the Scenes | What the Player Sees |
|---|---|
| Trust score with NPC drops by 15 | "Elena's expression hardens. She turns away without another word." |
| Courage attribute is 72, difficulty check is 60 | "You steady your breathing and step through the flames without hesitation." |
| Player's identity profile leans toward 'mercy' | A new dialogue option appears: "There's been enough blood today." |
| Stat check fails (roll above target) | "Your fingers slip on the wet stone, and the ledge crumbles beneath you." |

The player experiences cause and effect through characters, dialogue, and scene changes. The mechanical layer exists to produce believable, consistent narrative consequences, but it stays invisible.

### Why Fiction-First Matters for AI Content

AI-generated stories are especially vulnerable to feeling artificial when mechanics are exposed. A human author can sell "Trust -10" with voice and context; an AI model often cannot. By hiding the mechanics entirely:

- Awkward stat-change phrasing disappears
- The story reads like authored fiction, not a game log
- Players engage emotionally rather than analytically
- The "uncanny valley" of AI prose is minimized

### Anti-Goals

These are things the design actively avoids:

- **Visible RPG math:** No hit points, no experience bars, no damage numbers.
- **Choice-as-optimization:** Players should not be able to "solve" the story by picking the highest-stat option.
- **Flavor text without consequence:** Every meaningful choice must have real downstream effects, even if those effects are delayed.
- **Stat screens:** The player never sees their attribute values, skill numbers, or relationship scores.

## 3) Product Pillars

These five pillars define what the product must deliver. Every feature and system serves at least one pillar.

### Pillar 1: Read-First Play

**What it is:** The primary experience is reading prose, one beat at a time, in a full-screen, image-backed presentation. The app looks and feels like a cinematic visual novel.

**Why it matters:** Story comprehension and emotional pacing work best when the interface eliminates friction. Players should be absorbed in the narrative, not managing UI elements or navigating menus.

**How it manifests:**
- Beat-by-beat text display with a single tap/click to advance
- Full-screen background images that change with the story
- Minimal UI chrome (no persistent headers, sidebars, or stat panels)
- Dark gradient overlay on images ensures text readability
- Text sizing is adjustable for comfort

### Pillar 2: Choice-Driven Identity

**What it is:** The choices a player makes over the course of a story gradually build a hidden identity profile. The player is not choosing "Path A or Path B" — they are deciding what kind of person their character is becoming.

**Why it matters:** This transforms choices from a routing mechanism into a character-building experience. Over time, a pattern emerges: "I'm playing a merciful but reckless leader" or "I'm playing a calculating loner who keeps their word."

**How it manifests:**
- Six identity dimensions tracked behind the scenes (mercy vs. justice, cautious vs. bold, etc.)
- Dilemma choices that force value trade-offs
- Expression choices that establish personality and voice
- Identity-gated options that only appear if the player has established a strong enough trait
- NPCs who react to the player's accumulated identity, not just individual choices

### Pillar 3: Tactical Branching via Encounters

**What it is:** Encounters are high-intensity multi-beat sequences (combat, chase, negotiation, heist, etc.) where the player makes a series of tactical choices with visible momentum. Encounters create real divergence (different outcomes based on player actions) and then reconverge with the main plot.

**Why it matters:** Standard scene choices are reflective ("How do you respond?"). Encounters provide kinetic drama ("The guard is closing in — do you fight, hide, or bluff?"). They convert abstract stakes into visible tension and progression without requiring the entire story to branch permanently.

**How it manifests:**
- Clock systems (goal progress and threat escalation) inspired by the Blades in the Dark tabletop RPG
- Multi-phase encounters (setup, rising action, peak, resolution)
- Three outcome tiers per choice: success, complicated success, failure
- Approach system: aggressive, cautious, clever, desperate, adaptive
- Storylet aftermath sequences that are different depending on the encounter outcome

### Pillar 4: Structured AI Content Pipeline

**What it is:** Stories are generated by a team of specialized AI agents, each responsible for a different aspect of the content. The pipeline is sequenced, validated, and checkpointed.

**Why it matters:** Pure freeform generation (telling an AI "write me a story") produces inconsistent, meandering content. The structured pipeline ensures:
- World consistency (locations, factions, rules)
- Character depth (motivations, voice, relationships)
- Narrative pacing (encounter placement, choice density)
- Structural integrity (all links work, no dead ends, no orphaned scenes)

**How it manifests:**
- 10+ specialized agents (WorldBuilder, CharacterDesigner, StoryArchitect, SceneWriter, ChoiceAuthor, EncounterArchitect, etc.)
- Phase-by-phase execution with validation between phases
- Quality assurance checks for pacing, consequence coverage, NPC depth, and more
- Resumable jobs that survive failures and can be restarted

### Pillar 5: Persistent Creative Workspace

**What it is:** The generation process is treated as a creative workflow, not a one-shot request. Jobs persist, season plans are saved, generated stories accumulate, and the system supports iteration.

**Why it matters:** Generating a high-quality, multi-episode story can take 15-60 minutes and costs real money in API calls. The system must be resilient to failures, and users must be able to review, manage, and build on previous work.

**How it manifests:**
- Generation jobs tracked with progress, status, and estimated time remaining
- Season plans that outline multiple episodes with arc planning
- Checkpoint system for resuming interrupted generation
- Story library where generated and built-in stories coexist
- Settings for managing, deleting, and renaming stories

## 4) Player Experience Loop

### The Core Loop

The primary gameplay loop is straightforward:

1. **Choose a story** from the home screen (built-in or generated)
2. **Select an episode** within that story
3. **Read beats** — the story is presented one beat at a time with background images
4. **Make choices** at key moments — these shape the character and influence the narrative
5. **Experience consequences** — some are immediate (the scene reacts), others are delayed (choices echo back later)
6. **Complete the episode** — the story arc resolves, a cliffhanger may set up the next episode

### Loop Design Principles

- **Cadence over complexity:** Frequent small decisions keep engagement high. The player should encounter a choice every 60-90 seconds of reading.
- **Consequence layering:** Some outcomes are immediate ("The guard lets you pass"). Others return much later ("Three episodes later, the guard you spared warns you of an ambush"). This creates a sense that choices truly matter.
- **Clarity under uncertainty:** Players are given enough narrative context to roleplay their intent, but not enough mechanical information to solve choices by formula.

### The Secondary Loop: Content Generation

For users who generate their own stories, there is a secondary creative loop:

1. **Provide source material** (a document, a synopsis, or a brief description)
2. **Review the AI's analysis** of the source material
3. **Customize a season plan** (choose episode count, review episode outlines)
4. **Run generation** (the AI builds the full story content)
5. **Play the generated story** through the primary loop
6. **Provide feedback** (image regeneration, quality notes)

## 5) Narrative Architecture

### The Story Hierarchy

Every story follows a four-level hierarchy:

```
Story
  └── Episode (like a TV episode — self-contained arc with cliffhanger)
       └── Scene (a location or situation unit — "the tavern confrontation")
            └── Beat (a single moment — one paragraph of prose, one image, one choice)
```

#### Story

The top-level container. Contains metadata (title, genre, synopsis, cover image), initial player state, NPC definitions, and a list of episodes. A story might be a standalone one-episode tale or a multi-episode season.

#### Episode

Like an episode of a television show. Each episode has its own self-contained dramatic arc (beginning, middle, end) while contributing to the larger story. Episodes contain 5-8 scenes and end with either resolution or a cliffhanger.

#### Scene

A thematic unit that typically corresponds to a location, situation, or encounter. A scene contains a sequence of beats. A scene may optionally contain an encounter (a complex multi-beat interactive sequence). Scenes can be conditionally skipped (if the player hasn't met certain criteria), and scenes can route to different next scenes based on player state.

#### Beat

The atomic unit of content. A single beat is:
- One paragraph of narrative prose (typically 2-4 sentences)
- An optional background image
- An optional speaker (for dialogue beats)
- An optional set of choices

Beats are displayed one at a time. The player taps/clicks to advance to the next beat, or makes a choice if one is presented.

### Branching Strategy: Branch and Reconverge

Full open branching (where every choice leads to a completely different storyline) is exponentially expensive to generate and maintain. StoryRPG uses a "branch and bottleneck" strategy:

- **Branching moments** occur at specific, designed points (major choices, encounter outcomes). These create genuine divergence — the player sees different scenes, different dialogue, different consequences.
- **Bottleneck scenes** are convergence points where all paths must pass through. These are marked with the `isBottleneck` flag. The story reconverges at these points, but the reconvergence acknowledges the player's path (different dialogue, different NPC attitudes, different narrative tone based on how they arrived).
- **Controlled depth:** Branching is capped at a maximum of 2 branching choices per episode to prevent content explosion.

This means a player's experience feels meaningfully different based on their choices, but the content remains manageable for the AI to generate.

### Scene Routing

When a beat finishes and the player needs to move to the next scene, the engine uses a priority-based routing algorithm:

1. **Explicit choice routing** (highest priority): If the player just made a choice that specifies a `nextSceneId`, go to that scene.
2. **Conditional auto-routing:** If the current scene has a `leadsTo` list, evaluate each candidate scene's conditions. The first scene whose conditions the player satisfies is selected.
3. **Fallback chain:** If the target scene should be skipped (conditions not met), check its `fallbackSceneId` for an alternative.
4. **Sequential advancement:** If no routing rules apply, simply advance to the next scene in the episode's scene list.

### Storylets as Aftermath

After an encounter resolves, the outcome may trigger a "storylet" — a short, self-contained aftermath sequence (3-5 beats) that provides emotional payoff before the main plot resumes. Different encounter outcomes trigger different storylets:

- **Victory storylet:** The player succeeded — celebration, reward, or consequences of their decisive action.
- **Partial victory storylet:** Things went mostly right, but with complications.
- **Defeat storylet:** The player failed — consequences, regrouping, or a new problem introduced.
- **Escape storylet:** The player fled — safety, but at a cost.

Storylets prevent the jarring feeling of immediately returning to the main plot after an intense encounter.

## 6) Character System

### Player Character

The player character is defined by:

- **Name:** Chosen by the player or set by the story.
- **Pronouns:** he/him, she/her, or they/them. Used throughout the generated text via template substitution.
- **Attributes:** Six hidden stats that represent the character's core capabilities.
- **Skills:** Genre-specific abilities (e.g., "hacking" in a cyberpunk story, "sword_fighting" in fantasy).
- **Identity Profile:** Six moral/personality dimensions that emerge from accumulated choices.
- **Relationships:** Multi-dimensional connection scores with named NPCs.
- **Inventory:** Items acquired during the story.
- **Three-Layer State:** Flags (booleans), Scores (integers), and Tags (identity markers) for story-specific tracking.

### The Six Core Attributes

All attributes start at 50 (neutral) and range from 0-100. They are completely hidden from the player.

| Attribute | Description | When It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Charm** | Social magnetism and persuasion | Convincing NPCs, negotiation, first impressions |
| **Wit** | Cleverness and quick thinking | Puzzles, rapid decision-making, seeing through deception |
| **Courage** | Bravery and willingness to take risks | Facing danger, standing up to intimidation, bold actions |
| **Empathy** | Understanding others' emotions | Reading people, counseling, detecting lies through emotion |
| **Resolve** | Mental fortitude and determination | Enduring hardship, resisting temptation, staying focused |
| **Resourcefulness** | Problem-solving and adaptability | Improvisation, using environment, working with limited tools |

Attributes shift organically through player choices. A player who consistently makes brave decisions will see their Courage increase. The player never sees these numbers — they only experience the narrative effects.

### Skills

Skills are genre-specific and stored as key-value pairs (skill name → proficiency level). Examples:

- A heist story might include: lockpicking, surveillance, disguise, demolitions
- A fantasy story might include: sword_fighting, archery, herbalism, ancient_lore
- A modern thriller might include: hacking, marksmanship, interrogation, forgery

Skills contribute to stat check outcomes alongside attributes, making genre-appropriate competencies matter in encounters.

## 7) Choice System

### Choice Types

Every choice the player encounters falls into one of four categories. These categories describe the **player experience**, not the structural effect (any non-expression choice can potentially route to a different scene).

#### Expression Choices
- **Purpose:** Establish personality, voice, and tone.
- **Impact:** Cosmetic changes to how the character speaks or acts. Adds identity tags.
- **Branching:** Never branches the plot. Same scene continues regardless of choice.
- **Example:** When asked how you feel about a dangerous mission: "I've been waiting for this" vs. "I'll do it, but I don't have to like it" vs. "Just point me at the problem."

#### Relationship Choices
- **Purpose:** Shape connections with NPCs.
- **Impact:** Shifts trust, affection, respect, or fear dimensions of NPC relationships.
- **Branching:** May optionally route to a different scene.
- **Example:** When an ally confesses a secret: "I won't judge you" (trust +, affection +) vs. "You should have told me sooner" (trust -, respect +) vs. "I need time to think" (neutral, cautious).

#### Strategic Choices
- **Purpose:** Apply skill and competence to a practical problem.
- **Impact:** Often involves stat checks that determine success, complicated success, or failure.
- **Branching:** May optionally route to a different scene.
- **Example:** Breaking into a warehouse: "Pick the lock" (requires resourcefulness) vs. "Find a back entrance" (requires wit) vs. "Bluff the guard" (requires charm).

#### Dilemma Choices
- **Purpose:** Force a value conflict with no clearly right answer.
- **Impact:** Strongest identity shifts. Sets "tint flags" that push identity dimensions.
- **Branching:** May optionally route to a different scene.
- **Example:** A captured enemy begs for mercy, but letting them go risks your mission: "Show mercy" (sets tint:mercy) vs. "Justice demands accountability" (sets tint:justice) vs. "This isn't about morals — we can't risk it" (sets tint:pragmatism).

### Target Choice Distribution

Each episode targets a mix of choice types:
- 20% Expression (personality)
- 25% Relationship (social)
- 30% Strategic (competence)
- 25% Dilemma (values)

### Choice Mechanics

Each choice can optionally include:

- **Conditions:** Requirements to see the choice (attribute thresholds, flags, items, identity conditions). Unmet conditions either hide the choice or show it as locked (greyed out with a hint about what's required).
- **Stat Check:** A difficulty-based resolution that determines which outcome tier (success, complicated, failure) applies. The roll, target number, and probability are all hidden from the player.
- **Consequences:** Immediate state changes (attribute shifts, relationship changes, flag/score/tag updates).
- **Delayed Consequences:** "Butterfly effect" consequences that fire later (after N scenes/episodes, or when a specific condition is met).
- **Outcome Texts:** Three narrative variants (success, partial, failure) that describe what happens. Authored during generation to ensure prose quality.
- **Reaction Text:** A brief follow-up showing the world's immediate response.
- **Tint Flag:** An identity marker (e.g., `tint:mercy`, `tint:boldness`) that pushes the player's identity profile in a specific direction.
- **Scene Routing:** An optional `nextSceneId` that sends the player to a different scene (creating a branch).

## 8) Encounter System

### What Are Encounters?

Encounters are the high-intensity, multi-beat interactive sequences that serve as the dramatic centerpieces of episodes. While standard story scenes are reflective (read, consider, choose), encounters are kinetic (act, react, escalate, resolve).

Every episode is designed around at least one major encounter. Encounters can be combat, chase, heist, negotiation, investigation, survival, social confrontation, puzzle, exploration, stealth, or a mix.

### Encounter Structure

An encounter consists of:

1. **Clocks** (momentum tracking)
2. **Phases** (dramatic progression)
3. **Beats** (individual action moments)
4. **Choices** (tactical decisions with pre-generated outcomes)
5. **Environmental Elements** (dynamic hazards and opportunities)
6. **NPC States** (adversary behavior and tells)
7. **Escalation Triggers** (automatic complications)
8. **Outcomes** (four possible endings with story consequences)

### The Clock System

Inspired by the Blades in the Dark tabletop RPG, encounters track momentum through clocks:

- **Goal Clock:** Represents the player's progress toward their objective. Example: "Escape the Manor" (6 segments). Each successful action fills segments.
- **Threat Clock:** Represents escalating danger. Example: "Guards Close In" (8 segments). Failed or complicated actions fill segments. When the threat clock fills, something bad happens.
- **Complication Clock (optional):** Tracks additional complications that may arise during the encounter.

Clocks are currently visible to the player as abstract progress indicators, with the threat clock optionally showing approximate levels ("manageable," "growing," "dangerous," "critical") rather than exact numbers.

### Encounter Phases

Encounters progress through four dramatic phases:

1. **Setup:** The situation is established. Lower tension. Wide camera angles.
2. **Rising:** Complications emerge. Stakes escalate. Camera tightens.
3. **Peak:** Maximum tension. Critical decisions. Dramatic close-ups.
4. **Resolution:** The outcome crystallizes. Consequences are felt.

Each phase contains encounter beats with tactical choices.

### Encounter Choices and Branching Tree

Within an encounter, each choice is an `EncounterChoice` with:

- **Text:** Short, action-oriented ("Swing at his head," "Dive behind the crates")
- **Approach:** The tactical style (aggressive, cautious, clever, desperate, adaptive)
- **Primary Skill:** Which skill or attribute influences the outcome
- **Three Pre-Generated Outcomes:** For each choice, the AI has already written what happens on success, complicated success, and failure

The critical innovation is the **branching tree structure**. Each outcome can contain a `nextSituation` — a new narrative setup with its own choices. This means success and failure lead to genuinely different subsequent situations, creating a tree of possibilities within the encounter:

```
Encounter Beat 1: "The guard spots you"
  ├── Choice A: "Fight" → success/complicated/failure
  │   ├── Success → "Guard is down, but you hear footsteps" → new choices
  │   ├── Complicated → "You're locked in a struggle" → new choices
  │   └── Failure → "The guard sounds the alarm" → new choices
  └── Choice B: "Bluff" → success/complicated/failure
      ├── Success → "Guard buys your story" → new choices
      ├── Complicated → "Guard is suspicious but listening" → new choices
      └── Failure → "Guard doesn't believe you" → new choices
```

This creates genuinely branching narratives within encounters while keeping the content bounded.

### Approach System

Each encounter choice specifies an approach that influences both the stat check and the narrative tone:

- **Aggressive:** Direct, forceful actions. Often uses Courage or physical skills.
- **Cautious:** Measured, risk-averse actions. Often uses Resolve or defensive skills.
- **Clever:** Indirect, creative solutions. Often uses Wit or technical skills.
- **Desperate:** High-risk, high-reward gambles. Often uses Resourcefulness or improvisation.
- **Adaptive:** Reading the situation and responding appropriately. Often uses Empathy or social skills.

Players discover their preferred approach through play, and the story learns to offer them more choices in their preferred style.

### Resolution and Aftermath

When an encounter resolves, one of four outcomes is triggered:

1. **Victory:** The player achieved their primary goal with minimal complications.
2. **Partial Victory:** The player achieved their goal but with significant costs or complications.
3. **Defeat:** The player failed to achieve their goal but survives to face the consequences.
4. **Escape:** The player abandoned their goal to avoid worse consequences.

Each outcome triggers a different aftermath storylet (see section 5) before the main plot resumes.

## 9) Identity and Progression

### The Six Identity Dimensions

Player identity emerges through accumulated choices. Each dimension is a spectrum from -100 to +100, with values near 0 indicating the player hasn't established a strong position.

| Dimension | Description | Manifestation |
|---|---|---|
| **Mercy ↔ Justice** | How does the player approach moral violations? | Forgiveness vs. accountability, redemption vs. punishment |
| **Idealism ↔ Pragmatism** | How does the player approach difficult trade-offs? | Principles vs. results, purity vs. expedience |
| **Cautious ↔ Bold** | How does the player approach risk? | Planning vs. action, safety vs. opportunity |
| **Loner ↔ Leader** | How does the player relate to groups? | Independence vs. responsibility, self-reliance vs. delegation |
| **Heart ↔ Head** | How does the player make decisions? | Emotion vs. logic, intuition vs. analysis |
| **Honest ↔ Deceptive** | How does the player interact with others? | Truth vs. manipulation, openness vs. secrecy |

### Identity Gates

As a player establishes strong positions (values of ±35 or greater), new dialogue and action options become available:

- A merciful player (+35 mercy_justice) might see: "Everyone deserves a second chance."
- A pragmatic player (+35 idealism_pragmatism) might see: "We need to focus on what works."
- A bold player (+35 cautious_bold) might see: "Fortune favors the brave."

Identity gates create a sense that the character is growing and that their established personality matters.

### Growth Through Challenge

Attributes grow naturally through use:

- **Active Growth:** Successfully using an attribute in a stat check grants +1-3 points.
- **Passive Growth:** Failing a stat check grants +1 point (learning from failure).
- **Ceiling Effects:** Growth slows as attributes approach 80+.
- **Use-Based Training:** Skills improve when used successfully, especially on difficult challenges.

The player never sees numbers, but they experience their character becoming more competent over time.

### Relationship Progression

NPCs remember the player's actions and develop complex, multi-dimensional relationships:

- **Trust:** How reliable the player has been
- **Affection:** How much the NPC likes the player personally
- **Respect:** How much the NPC values the player's competence
- **Fear:** How much the NPC considers the player dangerous

These scores combine to create rich NPC behavior. An NPC might have high respect but low trust (they know you're competent but don't think you'll keep your word), or high affection but high fear (they like you but know you're dangerous).

## 10) Inventory and Items

### Fiction-First Items

Items in StoryRPG are narrative tools first, mechanical objects second. Every item has:

- **Name and Description:** What it is and what it looks like
- **Story Significance:** Why it matters to the narrative
- **Mechanical Effects:** Hidden stat bonuses, if any
- **Contextual Uses:** Specific story situations where it becomes relevant

### Item Categories

#### Tools and Equipment
Items that provide mechanical advantages in specific situations:
- Lockpicks (+resourcefulness when picking locks)
- Night vision goggles (+wit in dark environments)
- Fake credentials (+charm when bluffing authority)

#### Story Tokens
Items that unlock specific story beats or dialogue options:
- A letter from a dead friend (unlocks emotional conversation options)
- A faction insignia (grants access to restricted areas)
- A key or password (bypasses certain obstacles)

#### Identity Markers
Items that reflect and reinforce the player's identity:
- A weapon (suggests willingness to use violence)
- A medical kit (suggests compassionate preparation)
- A prayer book (suggests spiritual grounding)

### Hidden Mechanics

Players see their inventory as a list of items with descriptions. Behind the scenes:

- Items provide conditional stat bonuses
- Items can be consumed or broken during story events
- Items can be given to NPCs to improve relationships
- Items can be combined or transformed through story events

The mechanical layer stays invisible while providing meaningful advantages.

## 11) NPC and Relationship System

### NPC Depth

Every named NPC has:

- **Motivations:** What they want and why
- **Values:** What principles guide their decisions
- **Voice:** How they speak and express themselves
- **Relationship History:** How they've interacted with the player
- **Secrets:** Hidden information that may be revealed over time
- **Growth Arc:** How they might change over the course of the story

### Multi-Dimensional Relationships

Each NPC relationship is tracked across four dimensions:

#### Trust (-100 to +100)
How reliable the player has proven to be. Affected by:
- Keeping or breaking promises
- Sharing or withholding information
- Supporting the NPC in crucial moments

#### Affection (-100 to +100)
How much the NPC likes the player personally. Affected by:
- Sharing personal moments
- Showing understanding and empathy
- Making choices that align with the NPC's values

#### Respect (-100 to +100)
How much the NPC values the player's competence. Affected by:
- Succeeding at difficult challenges
- Making clever or insightful decisions
- Demonstrating relevant skills and knowledge

#### Fear (0 to +100)
How dangerous the NPC considers the player. Affected by:
- Using violence or threats
- Demonstrating powerful abilities
- Making choices that show ruthlessness

### Dynamic NPC Behavior

NPCs react to the player based on their accumulated relationship scores:

- **High Trust, High Affection:** "I'd follow you anywhere."
- **High Respect, Low Trust:** "You're brilliant, but I can't count on you."
- **High Fear, High Respect:** "You're terrifying, but I need you."
- **Low scores across the board:** "I don't really know what to make of you."

### NPC Memory and Consistency

NPCs remember past interactions and reference them in current conversations:

- "You helped me when no one else would."
- "Last time you promised to help, you disappeared."
- "I saw how you handled that situation with the guards."

This creates a sense of persistent relationships that grow and evolve over time.

## 12) Consequence System

### Immediate vs. Delayed Consequences

StoryRPG uses a dual consequence system:

#### Immediate Consequences
Effects that happen right after a choice:
- NPC reactions and dialogue changes
- Attribute or skill adjustments
- Flag or score updates
- Relationship shifts
- Scene routing changes

#### Delayed Consequences
Effects that happen later in the story:
- An NPC remembers your past choice and offers help
- A skill you developed becomes crucial in a future challenge
- A moral stance you took influences available options
- A relationship you built provides access to new information

### The Butterfly Effect Engine

Delayed consequences create the feeling that choices have long-term impact:

```typescript
interface DelayedConsequence {
  consequence: Consequence;
  description: string; // What happened, for the author's reference
  delay?: {
    type: 'scenes' | 'episodes';
    count: number;
  };
  triggerCondition?: ConditionExpression; // Fire when this becomes true
}
```

Examples:
- "Three episodes later, the guard you spared recognizes you and lets you pass."
- "When you next encounter a locked door, your improved resourcefulness gives you new options."
- "If you ever return to this city, the merchant you helped will offer you a discount."

### Consequence Validation

The AI pipeline includes validation checks to ensure consequences are:

- **Proportional:** Minor choices have minor consequences, major choices have major consequences
- **Coherent:** Consequences make narrative sense given the choice
- **Timed:** Delayed consequences trigger at dramatically appropriate moments
- **Connected:** Consequences reference specific past choices with enough detail for the player to remember

## 13) Visual Storytelling

### Image Integration Philosophy

Every story beat includes a background image that:

- **Sets the mood:** Color palette and lighting reflect emotional tone
- **Establishes location:** Players understand where they are without exposition
- **Shows character state:** Facial expressions and body language convey information
- **Supports narrative flow:** Visual transitions between beats create cinematic pacing

### The Visual Narrative System

Images are generated through a sophisticated pipeline:

1. **Drama Extraction:** Analyze the beat's text for emotional content, setting, characters present
2. **Cinematic Analysis:** Determine shot type (wide, medium, close-up) based on narrative function
3. **Character Consistency:** Ensure characters look the same across the story
4. **Composition Validation:** Check for visual coherence and readability
5. **Color Script Generation:** Create mood-appropriate color palettes
6. **Expression Validation:** Ensure character emotions match the text

### Character Reference Sheets

For each story, the system generates character reference sheets that define:

- Physical appearance (height, build, hair, clothing style)
- Personality indicators (posture, expression tendencies)
- Visual quirks (gestures, accessories, distinguishing features)
- Lighting preferences (how they look best in different moods)

### Dynamic Image Elements

Images respond to story state:

- **Character positioning:** Allies stand closer together as relationships improve
- **Environmental details:** Locations show wear, damage, or improvement based on player choices
- **Costume changes:** Characters' clothing reflects their emotional arc or status changes
- **Weather and atmosphere:** Environmental mood shifts to match narrative tone

## 15) Story Generation Workflow

### The Multi-Agent Pipeline

Story generation happens in phases, with specialized AI agents responsible for each aspect:

#### Phase 1: Foundation
- **SourceMaterialAnalyzer:** Extracts themes, characters, settings from input
- **WorldBuilder:** Creates world bible with rules, factions, locations
- **CharacterDesigner:** Designs NPCs with motivations, voices, relationships

#### Phase 2: Structure
- **SeasonPlannerAgent:** Plans episode outlines and story arcs along the 3-act / 7-point spine
- **StoryArchitect:** Creates scene-by-scene structure with branching points and scene blueprints
- **ThreadPlanner:** Authors the NarrativeThread ledger for setup/payoff and delayed consequences
- **TwistArchitect:** Schedules each episode's reversal/revelation and its foreshadow beats
- **CharacterArcTracker:** Emits per-episode identity/relationship milestone targets
- **EncounterArchitect:** Designs multi-beat interactive sequences

#### Phase 3: Content
- **SceneWriter:** Generates narrative prose, beats, and dialogue for each scene (absorbs the former BeatWriter / DialogueSpecialist / ScriptCompiler / ResolutionDesigner roles)
- **ChoiceAuthor:** Writes meaningful choices with multiple outcome tiers and inline resolution checks
- **SceneCritic (optional):** Rewrites scenes for subtext and reversals when enabled

#### Phase 4: Polish
- **QAAgents:** Validate pacing, consequence coverage, character voice, tone, sensitivity, continuity
- **ArcDeltaValidator + CharacterArcTracker:** Ensure start-vs-end identity/relationship deltas match the planned arc (replaces the old Blueprint/Growth Narrative Critics)
- **BranchManager:** Verify all story paths are complete and tested

#### Phase 5: Assets
- **VisualIllustratorAgent:** Generate story-consistent artwork for each beat
- **VideoDirectorAgent:** Create cinematic sequences for key moments
- **ColorScriptAgent:** Ensure visual consistency and mood progression

### Quality Assurance Philosophy

Every generated story passes through validation checks:

- **Structural integrity:** No broken links, orphaned scenes, or dead ends
- **Character consistency:** NPCs maintain coherent personalities and motivations
- **Consequence coverage:** Meaningful choices have appropriate downstream effects
- **Pacing validation:** Proper density of choices, encounters, and quiet moments
- **Growth opportunities:** Players can develop attributes and skills through play

### Iterative Improvement

The generation pipeline supports:

- **Checkpoint resumption:** Restart from any phase if generation fails
- **Selective regeneration:** Redo specific scenes or beats without rebuilding everything
- **Quality feedback:** User ratings and feedback inform future generation
- **Content refinement:** Manual editing and adjustment of generated content

## 16) Season Planning and Long-Form Content

### Episode Structure

Stories are organized into seasons (collections of episodes), where each episode:

- **Self-contained arc:** Beginning, middle, end within the episode
- **Season contribution:** Advances the larger story and character relationships
- **Player growth:** Provides opportunities to develop attributes, skills, and relationships
- **Cliffhanger potential:** May end with hooks for the next episode

### Seasonal Arc Planning

The SeasonPlannerAgent creates coherent multi-episode arcs:

- **Character development:** NPCs grow and change over time
- **Relationship evolution:** Player-NPC relationships deepen and shift
- **Escalating stakes:** Each episode raises the dramatic stakes
- **Payoff planning:** Early choices echo back in later episodes
- **Convergence points:** Multiple storylines come together at key moments

### Episode Pacing

Each episode targets:
- **15-25 minutes** of reading time
- **12-18 meaningful choices**
- **1-2 major encounters**
- **3-5 relationship moments**
- **1 significant consequence** from previous episodes

### Cross-Episode Continuity

The system maintains continuity through:

- **Player state persistence:** All choices and consequences carry forward
- **NPC memory:** Characters remember past interactions across episodes
- **World state tracking:** Locations and situations reflect past player actions
- **Relationship arcs:** Multi-episode character development and romance options
- **Skill progression:** Abilities improve through use across multiple episodes

StoryRPG focuses on:

- **Single-player experiences** with AI-generated narrative content
- **Choice-driven storytelling** with meaningful consequences
- **Identity development** through accumulated player decisions

## Conclusion

StoryRPG represents an experiment in AI-assisted interactive fiction. By combining the emotional depth of visual novels with the systemic richness of RPGs, and filtering everything through a fiction-first design philosophy, it aims to create a new form of digital storytelling.

The success of this approach depends on solving the central challenge: making AI-generated content feel authored and intentional rather than random and artificial. Through structured generation, hidden mechanics, and careful attention to narrative craft, StoryRPG attempts to bridge the gap between computational creativity and human storytelling.

The design remains experimental and evolving. Each generated story and player session provides data about what works, what doesn't, and where the technology needs to improve. The goal is not to replace human authors but to augment human creativity with computational tools that can generate coherent, engaging interactive narratives at scale.
