# StoryRPG Treatment Prompt - SceneEpisode Version

**Purpose:** Use this prompt guide when workshopping a StoryRPG season made of **sceneEpisodes**. A sceneEpisode is a small playable installment shaped like one strong dramatic scene, not a multi-scene television episode. It still belongs to a season, an act, an arc, and the season Story Circle spine, but its internal job is tighter: one entry goal, one central obstacle, one forced choice, one exit shift, and one clear consequence.

This document is for creating a **treatment**, not playable prose. The output should give the StoryRPG generator the raw material it needs: protagonist psychology, season structure, sceneEpisode sequencing, arcs, stakes, information movement, choice pressure, consequences, visual specificity, and exactly 3 alternate season endings.

## What Is A SceneEpisode?

A sceneEpisode is a playable unit that behaves like a complete dramatic scene:

- The protagonist enters with intent.
- Something blocks the goal.
- Pressure forces a choice.
- The protagonist exits on different emotional, strategic, relational, informational, or identity footing.

It is not:

- a full multi-scene episode
- a chapter summary
- a lore entry
- a transition
- a beat with a title
- filler between "real" episodes

Each sceneEpisode must produce consequence, reversal, discovery, cost, escalation, or choice residue. If it only says "and then this happened," it is not doing enough.

## Core StoryRPG Model

StoryRPG is choose-your-own-adventure / branching interactive fiction with visual-novel presentation and RPG-like consequence tracking hidden under fiction-first prose.

The player experiences:

- illustrated story moments
- protagonist-centered sceneEpisodes
- meaningful choices
- immediate and delayed consequences
- alternate paths that reconverge without erasing residue
- relationship, identity, information, resource, and reputation pressure
- exactly 3 alternate season endings based on repeated choice patterns

Never design around visible stats, dice, meters, levels, thresholds, builds, or optimization language. Mechanics should appear as fear, leverage, trust, injury, debt, access, temptation, preparation, reputation, information, relationship state, identity pressure, or resource pressure.

## Structural Hierarchy

Use this hierarchy for sceneEpisodes:

```text
Season
  Acts
    Arcs
      Seven-point anchors + buffers
        SceneEpisodes
          Beats
```

Each layer has a different job:

| Layer | Job |
|---|---|
| Season | The complete story unit. It has one season dramatic question framed around the protagonist's Lie. |
| Act | A broad season phase: setup/disruption, escalation/transformation, confrontation/aftermath. Acts are not arcs. |
| Arc | A cluster of sceneEpisodes that forms a pressure movement inside the season. For sceneEpisode seasons, an arc is usually 5-12 sceneEpisodes, or fewer in a short season. |
| Seven-point anchor | A required season-level structural turn carried by a sceneEpisode or small cluster of sceneEpisodes. |
| Buffer | Purposeful connective pressure between anchors. Buffers are not filler. |
| SceneEpisode | One playable dramatic scene with a complete scene-level turn. |
| Beat | The smallest visible story movement inside the sceneEpisode. |

The seven points are still the **season spine**, not a template repeated inside every sceneEpisode. However, every sceneEpisode must have its own mini dramatic structure, and that structure should reinforce the larger order of magnitude:

```text
beat pressure -> sceneEpisode shift -> arc turn -> act movement -> season transformation
```

## Story Logic Rule: No "And Then"

SceneEpisode seasons are especially vulnerable to feeling like a sequence of events. Avoid that by connecting every unit through **consequence, reversal, discovery, cost, escalation, or choice residue**, not chronology.

Weak chain:

```text
They visit the market, and then they meet the informant, and then they go to the tower.
```

Strong chain:

```text
The protagonist bargains at the market, therefore the informant agrees to talk.
But the bargain exposes a family debt.
Because the debt is exposed, the tower guards know what to threaten.
The next sceneEpisode begins with the cost of that choice already active.
```

Each sceneEpisode ending should create the reason the next sceneEpisode exists.

## Season Shape For SceneEpisodes

SceneEpisode seasons work best when the season is planned as a chain of scene-level turns rather than a list of mini-episodes.

Recommended scale:

- **Short season:** 8-12 sceneEpisodes
- **Standard season:** 12-20 sceneEpisodes
- **Long season:** 20-30 sceneEpisodes

Use a smaller scale when the premise is intimate, contained, or highly choice-dense. Use a larger scale when the season has multiple factions, slow-burn relationships, mystery runway, or several arcs.

### Story Circle Distribution

Each Story Circle beat can be carried by one sceneEpisode or a small cluster:

- **You:** 1 sceneEpisode
- **Need:** 1 sceneEpisode, sometimes paired with the preceding pressure sceneEpisode
- **Go:** 1 sceneEpisode
- **Search:** 1-2 sceneEpisodes, because adaptation may need discovery plus choice
- **Find:** 1-2 sceneEpisodes, because recontextualization may need discovery plus choice
- **Take:** 1 sceneEpisode
- **Return:** 1-2 sceneEpisodes, because choice and consequence may need separate playable moments
- **Change:** 1 sceneEpisode

Expansion sceneEpisodes should do specific work:

- consequence processing
- relationship fallout
- investigation
- preparation
- recovery
- alliance pressure
- information touch
- setup/payoff
- alternate leverage
- identity pressure
- cost of success

Buffers are not "quiet scenes." They are pressure scenes with a different kind of pressure.

## Acts, Arcs, And SceneEpisodes

Do not let these concepts fight.

- **Act:** a broad season phase.
- **Arc:** a pressure movement inside the season.
- **SceneEpisode:** one dramatic scene inside an act and arc.

For sceneEpisode seasons:

- An act contains multiple sceneEpisodes.
- An arc usually spans 5-12 sceneEpisodes.
- A sceneEpisode can function like a tiny act-out because its ending must turn the story.
- An arc finale resolves the arc question but hands pressure forward.
- The season finale resolves the season question and integrates aftermath.

The arc does not replace the Story Circle spine. The arc is how a run of sceneEpisodes pressures a facet of the season question.

## Season Dramatic Question

The season needs one central question framed around the protagonist's Lie.

Examples:

- "Can someone who believes love is transactional accept loyalty that cannot be bought?"
- "Will someone who survives by being invisible choose to be seen when others need them?"
- "Can the protagonist save their family without becoming the thing that wounded them?"

Do not state the theme as a noun. "Power" is not a theme. "What does power cost when the only way to keep it is to betray the people who gave it meaning?" is a theme question.

## Character Architecture

### Protagonist

The protagonist must have:

- **Want:** the conscious external goal.
- **Need:** what they actually need to recognize, accept, change, or refuse.
- **Lie:** the false belief that protects them and limits them.
- **Wound:** the past event or pattern that made the Lie useful.
- **Truth:** what growth would require them to recognize, or what they refuse in a tragic arc.
- **Arc mode:** positive, tragic, corruption, disillusionment, recovery, or ambiguous.
- **Climax choice:** the active choice that integrates the Truth or recommits to the Lie.
- **Pressure points:** relationships, fears, loyalties, temptations, secrets, debts, resources, status, or identity positions that sceneEpisode choices can test.

Every important sceneEpisode should press the Want/Need/Lie gap in some visible way.

### Supporting Characters

Major supporting characters need:

- Want
- leverage
- micro-Lie or contradiction
- pressure point
- secret or withheld information
- offscreen motivation or plan
- relationship to protagonist
- how choices can change trust, intimacy, hostility, access, secrets, or future help

Because StoryRPG is protagonist-facing, supporting characters do not need scenes without the protagonist. Their independent motivations happen offscreen and surface through:

- changed behavior
- contradictions
- missing information
- delayed reveals
- rumors
- new leverage
- altered access
- pressure from another faction
- a consequence the protagonist did not witness but now must handle

This is how B-plots work in sceneEpisode seasons: secondary character pressure intersects the protagonist's scenes rather than cutting away to scenes without the protagonist.

## A/B/C Pressure In SceneEpisodes

SceneEpisode seasons can still use A/B/C architecture, but the scale changes:

- **A pressure:** the main external story pressure.
- **B pressure:** a relationship, character, identity, or trust line that thematically rhymes with A.
- **C seed:** a future sceneEpisode/episode seed, clue, tonal counterweight, callback, object, image, rumor, debt, or unresolved pressure.

Rules:

- B-plots can be individual sceneEpisodes or recurring pressure inside multiple sceneEpisodes.
- C-plots are usually seeds for future sceneEpisodes, arcs, or episodes, not fully developed parallel plots.
- A and B should intersect or resonate at arc turns and major anchor sceneEpisodes.
- Since the protagonist is present in every sceneEpisode, secondary character stories are revealed through what the protagonist sees, triggers, learns, interrupts, misreads, or causes.

## Stakes Architecture

Strong sceneEpisodes stack multiple stakes layers:

| Layer | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Material | Money, job, possessions, position, access, tools, shelter, safety resources. |
| Relational | Trust, intimacy, family bonds, friendships, alliances, loyalty, betrayal. |
| Identity | Who the protagonist becomes if they succeed or fail. |
| Existential | Survival of self, others, a community, a way of life, or something meaningful. |

Rules:

- Major sceneEpisodes should usually stack at least 3 layers.
- Smaller bridge sceneEpisodes may carry 2 layers if they create clear consequence or setup.
- Establish personal stakes before expanding to existential stakes.
- Do not jump stakes tiers without earning investment.
- Within a sceneEpisode, beats should raise what is at risk slightly until the forced choice or climax beat.
- Escalation must grow from character choice, prior consequences, antagonist pressure, discovery, cost, relationship change, or revealed information.

## Theme Rules

Use theme as an active question:

- The theme must be a question, not a topic.
- The protagonist's choices must be able to answer or refuse the question.
- Each arc should press a different facet of the theme question.
- Each sceneEpisode should press, complicate, embody, test, or reframe the question.
- Never have characters state the theme as thesis dialogue.
- If a sceneEpisode does not press on the theme question or create story consequence, rewrite or cut it.

## Information Management For SceneEpisodes

For every key piece of information, decide its state:

- **Shared:** audience/player and protagonist know.
- **Withheld:** audience/player does not yet know.
- **Selective:** some characters know, others do not.

Rules:

- Hard cap: no more than 3 active mystery/box questions per season.
- Plan answers before introducing mysteries.
- Important payoffs need runway: 5-8 sceneEpisodes ahead.
- Major plants should be touched at least twice before payoff when scale allows.
- Each season should close more major questions than it opens, on net.
- Each sceneEpisode should usually perform one primary information job: plant, touch, reveal, pay off, close, or sharpen.
- Avoid stacking too many reveals in one sceneEpisode unless it is a major anchor.
- Surprise is best saved for anchor sceneEpisodes, arc finales, and season finale turns.
- Suspense often beats surprise: showing the threat creates longer tension than springing it from nowhere.

## Arc-Level Rules For SceneEpisodes

An arc is a run of sceneEpisodes with a distinct pressure question inside the season.

Each arc needs:

- **Arc dramatic question:** related to but distinct from the season question.
- **Relation to season question:** how this arc pressures a specific facet of the protagonist's Lie.
- **SceneEpisode turnout chain:** each sceneEpisode ending escalates, reverses, reveals, costs, or makes something irreversible.
- **Midpoint recontextualization:** the middle changes the question being asked, not just the intensity.
- **Late-arc crisis:** around 2/3 through, the protagonist appears to have failed or lost the old path.
- **Earned escalation:** each sceneEpisode leaves the protagonist worse off, more compromised, closer to truth, or knowing something they cannot unknow.
- **Arc finale:** resolves the arc question and hands pressure to the next arc without season-level finality.

If a sceneEpisode can move three places later without changing anything, the arc is slack.

## SceneEpisode Contract

Every sceneEpisode must satisfy all four core requirements:

- **Entry goal:** the protagonist enters with intent.
- **Obstacle:** something blocks the goal.
- **Choice forced:** the protagonist must decide, reveal, risk, withhold, concede, confront, sacrifice, or commit.
- **Exit shift:** the protagonist leaves on different emotional, strategic, relational, informational, or identity footing.

Additional requirements:

- Multi-character sceneEpisodes should shift power at least once.
- The sceneEpisode must connect to adjacent sceneEpisodes by consequence, reversal, discovery, cost, escalation, or choice residue.
- If it can be removed without narrative consequence, cut or rewrite it.
- Use subtext over direct explanation. Characters should rarely say exactly what they mean.
- Keep it as long as needed and no longer. Cut to the chase; leave on the punch.

SceneEpisode planning targets:

- **1 central pressure situation**
- **1 entry goal**
- **1 main obstacle**
- **1 forced choice or decisive pressure turn**
- **1 clear exit shift**
- **1-2 meaningful choices**
- **3-8 beats**
- **1 primary information job**
- **1 visible consequence or setup/payoff movement**

## Branching And Agency

Player agency should influence direction without creating infinite branching.

Rules:

- Aim for 60%+ of major plot turns to be caused by protagonist/player action, preparation, relationship leverage, sacrifice, refusal, or choice.
- Use branch-and-bottleneck structure: real divergence, planned reconvergence, persistent residue.
- Branches can last one sceneEpisode, several sceneEpisodes, an arc segment, or carry into ending eligibility.
- Branches should change outcome, process, information, relationship, identity, resource, leverage, reputation, or ending eligibility.
- Reconvergence must not erase the path. It should retain altered dialogue, trust, help, scars, missing information, debts, or changed access.
- Prefer callbacks and scene tints for most consequences.
- Use branchlets for major sceneEpisode choices.
- Reserve large structural branches for arc finales, climax clusters, and endings.

Failure should create story, not a dead end:

- preparation
- training
- recovery
- mentorship
- alliance
- investigation
- information gathering
- alternate leverage
- harder route through another relationship or resource

## Visual Specificity

Be visually specific in story terms, not art-direction terms.

Useful:

- silhouettes
- clothing
- physical markers
- wounds
- props
- gestures
- spatial relationships
- environmental pressure
- repeated objects
- visible consequences
- key images

Avoid generic style words unless the user explicitly requests that style:

- cinematic
- hyperreal
- dramatic lighting
- painterly
- anime
- gritty
- glossy
- high contrast
- vivid colors

### 11. Capability, Growth, And Fail-Forward

- Key challenges that test skill, relationships, identity, resources, prior choices, or encounter outcomes
- How failure opens preparation, recovery, alliance, investigation, information, alternate leverage, or a harder route
- Which sceneEpisodes show growth through changed behavior rather than visible stats
- How growth remains fiction-first without visible stats, levels, thresholds, or grind language

### 12. SceneEpisode Endings

For each non-finale sceneEpisode ending:

- What immediate pressure closes or changes
- What cost, reveal, danger, relationship rupture, choice consequence, or pressure carries forward
- Why this is not fake withholding
- What specific next sceneEpisode pressure it creates

For finale/resolution sceneEpisodes:

- What central conflict resolves
- What was saved or lost
- What the protagonist becomes
- What cost remains
- What future or legacy is implied

### 13. Alternate Endings

Provide exactly 3 alternate season ending targets.

For each:

- Name
- Summary
- Emotional register
- Theme payoff
- State drivers
- Target conditions in plain language
- What repeated choice pattern this ending pays off
- Which sceneEpisode choices or residues most influence eligibility

The three endings should be distinct emotional and thematic destinations, not success/neutral/failure reskins.

### 14. Failure Mode Audit

Before finalizing, audit for:

- **Escalation trap:** stakes rise faster than investment.
- **Mystery box collapse:** questions pile up without planned answers.
- **Character drift:** actions contradict established psychology without earned change.
- **Shaggy dog:** large setup receives weak payoff.
- **Passive protagonist:** events happen to the lead more than the lead causes them.
- **Reset disease:** sceneEpisodes restore the prior state.
- **Theme drift:** sceneEpisodes do not press on the theme question.
- **Unmotivated escalation:** threat rises because the writer wants it to, not because the situation demands it.
- **Snowglobe arcs:** arc end restores arc beginning.
- **Inverted thematic rhyme:** B pressure has no relation to A pressure.
- **Convenient coincidence:** rescue, luck, prophecy, or outside action solves the problem.
- **Telegraphed twist:** clue language makes the reveal obvious.
- **Cheating twist:** reveal has no fair setup.
