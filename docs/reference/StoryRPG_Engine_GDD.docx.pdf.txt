1. Executive Summary
StoryRPG Engine is a mobile interactive storytelling platform that combines the accessible,
episodic format of apps like Choices and Episodes with meaningful RPG progression
mechanics and AI-generated content at scale. By leveraging pre-generated AI stories and
images, the platform delivers personalized, deeply branching narratives that would be
prohibitively expensive to produce through traditional methods.
Core Value Proposition
• For Players: Experience stories where your choices genuinely matter, your character
grows across seasons, and the narrative remembers and responds to everything you do.
• For the Business: AI-generated content enables complex branching structures at a
fraction of traditional production costs, allowing for higher content volume and deeper
personalization.
• Differentiation: Unlike competitors that use linear stories with cosmetic choices,
StoryRPG Engine delivers true RPG progression where stats, items, and relationships
create meaningfully different experiences.
Key Features
• Multi-genre library supporting romance, fantasy, thriller, sci-fi, horror, and more
• Hybrid stat system combining social attributes, skills, and relationship meters
• Soft influence mechanics where stats affect probability of success, not hard locks
• Functional and cosmetic item systems that unlock scenes and customize appearance
• Quality-based narrative structure enabling true personalization without combinatorial
explosion
• AI pre-generated stories and art with consistent quality and style per series

2. Core Concept & Vision
2.1 Vision Statement
Create the most emotionally rewarding interactive storytelling experience on mobile by
combining the fiction-first design philosophy with meaningful player progression and AI-enabled
narrative complexity.
2.2 Design Pillars
Fiction-First Design
Every mechanic exists to serve the narrative. Stats are not abstract numbers but
representations of who your character is becoming. Items are not inventory management but
story keys that unlock scenes, solve problems, and deepen relationships. The interface
disappears into the drama.
Meaningful Agency
Choices must change at least one of: outcome (what happens), process (how it happens),
information (what you learn), relationship (who trusts you), or identity (who you are becoming). If
none of these change, the choice is removed.
Earned Progression
Character growth should feel earned, not purchased. Free players can complete any story;
premium options enhance the experience but never gate core narrative content.
Emotional Resonance
Every season should leave players with a memorable emotional experience. The goal is not just
entertainment but stories that stay with players after they put down their phones.

3. Target Audience
3.1 Primary Demographic
• Age: 25-45 years old
• Gender: All genders, with content variety to appeal broadly
• Gaming Experience: Casual gamers comfortable with mobile interfaces
• Reading Habits: Enjoys fiction, podcasts, streaming series, or audiobooks
• Available Time: 10-20 minutes per session, typically commute, lunch, or evening
wind-down
3.2 Player Personas
The Story Seeker
Primarily motivated by narrative quality and emotional engagement. Values character
development, plot twists, and meaningful consequences. Less interested in min-maxing stats
but appreciates when choices feel impactful.
The Completionist
Wants to see all content, collect all items, and explore every branch. Motivated by achievement
systems and unlockables. Will replay stories to see different outcomes and is likely to spend on
content bundles.
The Role-Player
Creates a headcanon for their character and makes choices based on that identity rather than
optimal outcomes. Values expression choices and relationship dynamics. Deeply invested in
their avatar and customization options.
3.3 Accessibility Considerations
• Adjustable text size and font options
• Color-blind friendly UI with icon-based indicators
• Optional audio narration for vision-impaired users
• Dyslexia-friendly font option
• Content warnings and skip options for sensitive material

Relationship Meters
Each significant NPC has multiple tracked dimensions:
• Trust: How much they believe in you (affects information sharing, assistance)
• Affection: Romantic/platonic closeness (affects intimacy scenes, loyalty)
• Respect: Professional/personal regard (affects their deference to your decisions)
• Fear: Intimidation level (affects compliance, but damages long-term relationship)
5.2 Stat Progression (Hidden System)
Character growth happens organically through story choices and actions. Players never see XP
bars, level-ups, or point allocation screens. Instead, stats evolve based on how players engage
with the narrative.
Internal Progression Triggers
• Choosing brave actions consistently increases Courage behind the scenes
• Successfully navigating social situations builds Charm
• Solving puzzles or noticing details improves Wit
• Showing compassion and reading emotions develops Empathy
• Standing firm under pressure strengthens Resolve
• Finding creative solutions enhances Resourcefulness
Narrative Stat Surfacing
Players discover their character's growth through fiction, not numbers:
• NPCs comment on changes: You seem more confident lately or You've become quite the
smooth talker
• Internal monologue reflects growth: A year ago, this would have terrified me. Now I
barely flinch.
• New dialogue options appear naturally without stat displays
• The Character Sheet shows narrative descriptors, not numbers (e.g., Courageous rather
than Courage: 14)
5.3 Fiction-First Resolution
StoryRPG Engine uses fiction-first resolution inspired by Powered by the Apocalypse design.
The fiction triggers the roll; the roll triggers new fiction. Players never see dice, percentages, or
success/failure labels.

Core Philosophy
• The Fiction Comes First: Mechanics only engage when the narrative demands
uncertainty. If the fiction establishes you can do something, you do it. Rolls happen when
outcomes are genuinely uncertain and dramatically interesting.
• Rolls Create Fiction: The result of a roll is not pass/fail but what happens next. Every
outcome moves the story forward in a compelling direction.
• No Visible Math: Players see narrative outcomes, not numbers. The system handles
probability invisibly.

How Stats Influence Outcomes
Behind the scenes, the system uses player stats to weight probabilities:
• Base success chance determined by action difficulty (set by narrative designers)
• Relevant stats shift the probability curve (higher stats = more likely to roll into success
tiers)
• Relationship states can provide bonuses (an ally's trust grants advantage)
• Items can shift outcomes (possessing the right tool guarantees at least complicated
success)
Players never see these calculations. They experience the story.

Example: Fiction-First in Practice
Traditional RPG approach: [Charm 14] Persuade the guard - 75% success. You roll... Success!
Fiction-first approach: The guard's eyes narrow. You could try to talk your way past him—you've

plays out based on internal resolution: either the guard steps aside (success), lets you through
but remembers your face (complicated), or calls for backup while you slip away (interesting
failure). The player simply experiences the narrative result.

5.5 Complex Encounter System
Complex encounters—combat, chases, heists, debates, rituals, and other multi-beat dramatic
sequences—use a specialized system that maintains fiction-first principles while delivering
dynamic, visually rich experiences through pre-generated image sequences.
Page 13

Encounter Design Philosophy
• Cinematic, Not Tactical: Encounters feel like action scenes in a film, not turn-based
strategy. Players make dramatic choices, not optimal moves.
• Visually Dynamic: Pre-generated images create the illusion of real-time action.
Different outcomes trigger different image sequences, making each playthrough feel
unique.
• Narratively Integrated: Every encounter serves the story. Combat reveals character.
Chases raise stakes. The encounter is a story beat, not a minigame.
Encounter Structure
Complex encounters are divided into Beats—discrete dramatic moments where the player
makes a choice and the system resolves the outcome.

The Beat Cycle
Each beat within an encounter follows this cycle:
• 1. Situation Image: A pre-generated image shows the current state of the encounter
(enemy positions, environmental context, ally status).
• 2. Choice Moment: Player selects from 2-4 dramatically meaningful options. Choices
are fiction-first: Fight back, Protect your ally, Look for an escape, or Surrender.
• 3. Internal Resolution: System calculates outcome based on relevant stats, items,
relationship support, and encounter difficulty. Player never sees this.

Relationship Support in Encounters
Allies present during encounters can provide support based on relationship state:
• High Trust: Ally actively assists, providing stat bonuses to relevant actions
• High Affection: Ally protects you, absorbing complications that would harm you
• High Respect: Ally follows your lead, enabling coordinated actions
• Low Relationship: Ally may hesitate, act independently, or even work against you
These modifiers affect internal resolution but are expressed through fiction—ally dialogue,
actions shown in images, narrative descriptions.
Encounter Consequences
Encounters are not isolated—outcomes ripple through the story:
• Immediate: Character status changes (injured, exhausted, empowered), item
gains/losses, NPC status changes
• Relationship: How you behaved in the encounter affects relationship meters (did you
protect your ally? abandon them? use them as a distraction?)
• Narrative: Encounter outcomes can branch the story, lock/unlock future scenes, or
change NPC availability
• Stat Growth: Engaging with encounters in specific ways triggers internal stat
progression (fighting courageously builds Courage)

6. Narrative Design & Story Structure
6.1 Fiction-First Principles
Every design decision is evaluated against the fiction-first philosophy:
• Diegetic Integration: Stats and items are explained within the story world. Characters
comment on your growing confidence (Courage) or your new jacket (Cosmetic). Nothing
feels gamey.
• Consequence Primacy: Consequences are primarily fictional (who trusts you, what
scenes unlock) rather than numerical. Stats change, but the emotional weight comes
from narrative.
• Character Integrity: The protagonist has a defined core that players shape but do not
violate. Players choose HOW the character responds, not WHETHER they are
fundamentally the character.
6.2 Hybrid Narrative Structure
StoryRPG Engine uses a hybrid structure combining branch-and-bottleneck with quality-based
narrative (QBN) elements:
Season Architecture
• Season: One complete story arc (15-20 episodes), ending with all character paths
converging to a shared narrative state
• Episode: One play session (10-15 minutes), ending on a hook or cliffhanger
• Scene: One continuous narrative beat, potentially with multiple choice points
• Storylet: Modular scene unlocked by player state (stats, items, relationships)
The Convergent Spine
Each season has a defined starting state and ending state. While players can reach wildly
different experiences in between, all paths must deliver the character to the same narrative
position at season end. This enables:
• Sequel seasons that build on established foundations
• Complex mid-season branching without exponential growth
• Meaningful choices that affect HOW you arrive, not WHETHER you arrive

7. AI Content Generation Pipeline
7.1 Content Generation Philosophy
AI generation is a production tool, not a replacement for creative direction. All AI-generated
content goes through human curation before release. The goal is to enable complexity and
personalization at scale while maintaining quality standards.
7.2 Story Generation Pipeline
Phase 1: Story Bible Creation (Human)
• Creative directors define premise, themes, character arcs, and emotional beats
• Define the convergent spine: starting state, key milestones, ending state
• Establish tone, genre conventions, and content boundaries
• Create character profiles with voice samples and relationship dynamics
Phase 2: Scene Generation (AI + Human)
• AI generates scene variants based on story bible parameters
• Each scene produced in multiple versions for different stat/relationship states
• Human editors review, revise, and approve all generated content
• Quality assurance ensures consistency, pacing, and emotional impact
Phase 3: Integration (Human)
• Technical writers implement branching logic and variable tracking
• Playtesting validates all paths for coherence and quality
• Final polish pass for dialogue flow and pacing
7.3 Image Generation Pipeline

• Style guide defining character designs, color palette, mood

7.4 Encounter Image Pipeline
Complex encounters require the most intensive image generation. Each encounter follows a
structured pipeline:
Phase 1: Encounter Storyboarding
• Narrative designers define encounter beats, choices per beat, and possible outcomes
• Visual directors create shot lists specifying camera angles, character positions, action
types
• Consistency requirements documented (character appearance, environment, lighting
continuity)
Phase 2: Batch Generation
• Situation images generated first to establish visual baseline
• Outcome sequences generated in batches by resolution tier (all successes, then
complications, then failures)
• Character consistency enforced through fine-tuned models and reference images
Phase 3: Sequence Assembly
• Images organized into playable sequences
• Transition timing specified (how long each image displays)
• Animation hints added (pan direction, zoom, shake effects applied at runtime)

10. User Interface & Experience
10.1 Design Principles
• Invisible Interface: UI should disappear during story moments. Minimize chrome,
maximize narrative immersion.
• Contextual Surfacing: Show relevant information when needed, hide it otherwise. Stats
appear during stat checks, not constantly.
• Emotional Alignment: UI animations and sounds should match story tone. Tense
scenes get different treatment than romantic ones.
10.2 Core Screens
Library

16.2 Story Generation Pipeline
The story generation pipeline transforms a creative brief into playable episode content through a
series of agent handoffs.
Phase 1: Foundation (Human + AI)
Human creative directors provide the initial brief. AI agents expand and structure it.
• Input: Creative brief (genre, themes, tone, target length, unique hooks)
• Story Architect Agent: Generates the season spine—inciting incident, major plot beats,
midpoint, climax, resolution. Defines the convergent endpoints each episode must reach.
• World Builder Agent: Creates setting bible—locations, rules of the world,
technology/magic systems, social structures, history relevant to plot.
• Character Designer Agent: Develops core cast profiles—backstory, motivations, voice
samples, relationship dynamics, arc trajectories, visual description for art generation.
• Output: Story Bible document (50-100 pages equivalent) stored in vector database for
retrieval
• Human Checkpoint: Creative director reviews and approves/revises Story Bible before
proceeding
Phase 2: Episode Architecture (AI)
With approved Story Bible, agents generate episode-level structure.
• Branch Manager Agent: For each episode, defines the choice architecture—where
branches occur, what variables they set, how they reconverge. Outputs a visual node
graph.
• Scene Writer Agent: Generates scene-by-scene breakdown with dramatic purpose,
emotional beats, and pacing notes. Identifies which scenes need variants based on
player state.
• Variable Tracker Agent: Maintains the master variable registry—all flags, relationship
states, stat triggers. Ensures no orphaned variables or impossible states.
• Output: Episode blueprint with node graph, scene list, variable schema
• Human Checkpoint: Narrative designer reviews structure, approves choice architecture
Page 41

Game Design Document | Confidential
Phase 3: Content Generation (AI)
Agents generate the actual narrative content for each scene and variant.
• Scene Writer Agent: Writes full scene content including narration, action descriptions,
and scene-setting. References Story Bible via RAG for consistency.
• Dialogue Specialist Agent: Generates character dialogue, ensuring voice consistency.
Produces multiple variants for different relationship states (cold, neutral, warm,
romantic).
• Choice Author Agent: Writes choice text and immediate responses. Ensures choices
are meaningfully different and emotionally clear. Tags each choice with stat influences
and consequence tier.
• Output: Complete episode script in structured format (JSON/YAML) with all variants
• Human Checkpoint: Editor reviews dialogue quality, emotional beats, choice clarity
Phase 4: Polish & Integration (AI + Human)
• Continuity Checker Agent: Scans generated content against Story Bible and previous
episodes for contradictions.
• Tone Validator Agent: Ensures consistent tone across variants and scenes. Flags
jarring shifts.
• Script Compiler Agent: Converts approved content into game engine format. Validates
all variable references.
• Output: Production-ready episode file
• Human Checkpoint: Final read-through, QA playtest

16.3 Image Generation Pipeline
Visual content generation runs parallel to narrative generation, with tight integration for
character and scene consistency.
Phase 1: Visual Foundation
• Art Director Agent: Based on Story Bible, defines visual style guide—color palette,
lighting approach, composition rules, mood boards. Selects or creates base model/LoRA
for the series.
• Character Artist Agent: Generates character reference sheets—front, side, 3/4 views,
expression range (8+ expressions), key poses. These become the consistency anchors.
• Environment Artist Agent: Creates background art for all major locations. Generates
day/night and weather variants where needed.
• Output: Visual Bible—style guide, character sheets, location backgrounds
• Human Checkpoint: Art director approves visual direction, character designs
Phase 2: Scene Illustration
Once episode scripts are approved, scene illustrations are generated.
• Scene Illustrator Agent: For each scene requiring illustration, generates image based
on script description. Uses character reference sheets via IP-Adapter or similar for
consistency.
• Composition Validator Agent: Checks generated images against composition
rules—character placement, emotional tone match, readability at mobile resolution.
• Batch Processor: Generates multiple candidates per scene (3-5), ranks by quality
metrics, surfaces top options for review.
• Output: Scene illustrations matched to script
• Human Checkpoint: Art review for quality, consistency, emotional alignment
Phase 3: Character Expressions
• Expression Generator Agent: For dialogue-heavy scenes, generates character
expression variants matching emotional beats in script (happy, sad, angry, surprised,
etc.).
• Consistency Enforcer Agent: Compares generated expressions to character reference
sheet. Rejects images below similarity threshold. Triggers regeneration with adjusted
prompts.

16.4 Encounter Generation Pipeline
Complex encounters require the most intensive coordination between narrative and visual
agents.
Phase 1: Encounter Architecture
• Encounter Architect Agent: Based on episode blueprint, designs encounter
structure—number of beats, escalation curve, climax moment, resolution variants.
Defines which stats are relevant.
• Beat Writer Agent: For each beat, generates situation description, 2-4 choice options
with stat mappings, and three resolution outcomes (success, complicated, failure).
• Consequence Mapper Agent: Defines how each resolution affects subsequent beats.
Maps relationship changes, status effects, and narrative branches.
• Output: Encounter blueprint with full beat structure and resolution matrix
• Human Checkpoint: Narrative designer reviews encounter flow, dramatic pacing
Phase 2: Encounter Image Generation
This is the highest-volume image generation task, requiring careful orchestration.
• Storyboard Agent: Converts beat descriptions into shot-by-shot storyboards—camera
angle, character positions, action depicted, emotional tone for each image in every
sequence.
• Situation Image Generator: For each beat, generates the situation image showing the
encounter state before player choice.
• Sequence Generator Agent: For each choice × resolution tier, generates the 3-4 image
outcome sequence. Maintains visual continuity within sequence.
• Action Pose Library Agent: Maintains and extends library of character action poses.
Reuses poses across encounters where appropriate to reduce generation volume.
• Output: Complete image set for encounter (40-60 images per encounter)
• Human Checkpoint: Art review for sequence flow, action clarity, emotional impact
Phase 3: Sequence Assembly
• Sequence Compiler Agent: Organizes images into playable sequences with timing
data. Adds pan/zoom hints for runtime animation.
• Transition Designer Agent: Creates smooth visual transitions between beats. Ensures
narrative continuity across resolution → next situation.

• Playback Validator Agent: Simulates encounter playback, checking for visual jarring,
timing issues, or missing assets.
• Output: Encounter package ready for integration

Feedback Loop Architecture
When human reviewers request revisions, structured feedback flows back to agents:
• Reviewer provides feedback in structured format (category, severity, specific note,
suggested fix)
• Feedback Interpreter Agent parses notes into actionable instructions for generation
agents
• Relevant agent receives original content + feedback + instruction to revise
• Agent generates new version, explicitly addressing each feedback point
• Revision is logged with before/after comparison for learning
Parallel Processing Strategy
To maximize throughput, the pipeline supports parallel generation where dependencies allow:
• Multiple episodes can be in different pipeline stages simultaneously
• Visual generation begins as soon as character designs are approved (before script
completion)
• Scene illustrations and encounter images generate in parallel batches
• QA agents run asynchronously, flagging issues without blocking generation
• Human review queues prioritize blocking items to prevent pipeline stalls
State Persistence & Recovery
• Checkpointing: Full pipeline state saved after each stage transition. Enables recovery
from failures without restart.
• Version History: All generated content versioned. Previous versions retrievable for
comparison or rollback.
